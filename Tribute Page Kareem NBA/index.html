Kareem Abdul Jabbar

The Big 'A'

A picture of Kareem in a Lakers uniform

Kareem Abdul Jabbar (born Ferdinand Lewis Alcindor Jr 16th April 1947) is an former African-American professional basketball player who played 20 seasons in the National Basketball Association (NBA) for the Milwaukee Bucks and the Los Angeles Lakers. During his career as a center, Abdul-Jabbar was a record six-time NBA Most Valuable Player (MVP), a record 19-time NBA All-Star, a 15-time All-NBA selection, and an 11-time NBA All-Defensive Team member.

When Kareem Abdul-Jabbar left the game in 1989 at age 42, no NBA player had ever scored more points, blocked more shots, won more Most Valuable Player Awards, played in more All-Star Games or logged more seasons than Kareem.

No player in NBA history had more personal and team success in their career than Kareem Abdul Jabbar.

Personal Life

At age 24 in 1971, he converted to Islam and became Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, which means "noble one, servant of the Almighty."

Abdul-Jabbar had stated that he was "latching on to something that was part of my heritage, because many of the slaves who were brought here were Muslims.My people were Yoruba, and their culture survived slavery.

Playing in Los Angeles facilitated Abdul-Jabbar's trying his hand at acting. He made his film debut in Bruce Lee's 1972 film Game of Death, in which his character Hakim fights Billy Lo (played by Lee).

Abdul-Jabbar has had numerous other television and film appearances, often playing himself. He has had roles in movies such as Fletch, Troop Beverly Hills and Forget Paris, and television series such as Full House, Living Single, Amen, Everybody Loves Raymond, Martin and more.

Kareem Abdul Jabbar has a passion for writing and activism. Some of his notable works are Giant Steps(1983),Writings on the Wall: Searching for a New Equality Beyond Black and White with Raymond Obstfeld (2016) and Becoming Kareem: Growing Up On and Off the Court (2017)
